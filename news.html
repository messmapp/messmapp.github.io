Intense radiation fields, powerful jets and efficient accretion

Understanding the physics behind blazars that are putative candidates for the emission of IceCube neutrinos is essential to shed more light on the possible connection between these elusive particles and the extragalactic sources that may originate them. A work focused on the multi-wavelength properties of candidate neutrino-emitter blazars was led by Alessandra Azzollini, PhD student in the MessMapp group, and recently accepted in the Astronomy & Astrophysics Journal. The study investigates the inner nature of a subset of 52 blazars using radio, optical spectroscopic, and gamma-rays information. Furthermore, the work performs a statistical analysis to compare the properties of the target sample to those of reference blazar samples from the literature. The candidate neutrino-emitter blazars show a mild tendency toward intense radiation fields, powerful jets and radiatively efficient accretion, overall consistent with the reference samples. August 2025

X-rays - a key messenger in the multimessenger puzzle of blazars.

Considering lepto-hadronic modelling to explain the SED of blazars, X-rays have been shown to be a key component as they may trace hadronic emissions, hinting directly at neutrino production. In a new study accepted by The Astrophysical Journal, MessMapp PhD student Jose Maria Sanchez Zaballa investigated the blazar 5BZB J0630-2406, a candidate neutrino source, using multi-epoch observations from XMM-Newton, NuSTAR, Swift-XRT, and eROSITA. While blazars are typically dominated by jet emission, Jose Maria and collaborators discovered that during low-flux states, 5BZB J0630-2406 shows an unexpected hard X-ray component. This suggests that the source may switch from a jet-dominated regime to a more complex emission scenario, consistent with processes such as coronal emission or photoionized absorption usually found in obscured AGN. This work points out the connection between blazars and high-energy neutrinos, but also highlights the intricate interplay between jets and the nuclear environment at the heart of active galaxies.

ICRC 2025 - The Astroparticle Physics Conference

The 2025 International Cosmic Ray Conference took place in Geneva. Massimiliano Lincetto, postdoc in the MessMapp group, presented in a first talk the latest news on icecubepy, an open source software for the analysis of public IceCube data, fruit of an intense development effort. More news about IceCubePy will follow in the public GitHub repository, with a code release expected in the upcoming months (in the meantime, we are happy to share the code with interested collaborators).
In a second talk, Massimiliano reported on the search and characterisation of candidate blazar counterparts for the KM3NeT ultra-high energy event, that involved the MessMapp group together with several large international collaborations: check the pre-print on arXiv!

Fermi-LAT Collaboration Meeting 2025

At the 2025 Fermi-LAT Collaboration Meeting in Milan, Leonard Pfeiffer contributed with two talks. One presentation focused on the search for blazar counterparts to the recent KM3NeT ultra-high energy neutrino detection, building on the results reported in the pre-print available on arXiv. In a separate talk, he provided an overview of the progress on neutrino follow-ups with Fermi-LAT, showcasing how rapid response analyses are being developed to strengthen the connection between high-energy neutrino alerts and gamma-ray observations.

March 2025

Workshop on Numerical Multi-Messenger Modelling

This workshop took place at DESY Zeuthen, bringing together the community working on numerical codes to simulate multi-messenger emission (photons, neutrinos, cosmic rays) from astrophysical sources. The workshop was designed to foster discussion and co-working, with sessions dedicated to recent code developments and comparisons, applications to prominent sources in the photon/neutrino sky, and strategies for multi-messenger data fitting. A huge organizational effort by Margot Boughelilba made the event possible.
Our group contributed with several talks: Jose Maria Sanchez Zaballa presented results on X-ray spectral features and neutrino production in blazar 5BZB J0630-2406; Leonard Pfeiffer reported on the lepto-hadronic modelling of the neutrino-candidate blazar 5BZB J1150+2417; and Massimiliano Lincetto discussed the KM3NeT ultra-high-energy neutrino and its possible blazar origin (see also the pre-print on arXiv). To close, Sara Buson gave an overview of MessMapp activities and recent science highlights, placing the presented studies in a broader context and outlining the group's future directions.

The Highest Energy Neutrino Ever Observed

The KM3NeT collaboration measured a neutrino of record breaking energies using their ARCA detector in the mediterranian sea. Our team contributed to this study, helping shedding light in the possible blazar counterparts to this ultra-high-energy neutrino. Read more about these in the article published in Nature and the companion paper lead by our team. For more information, see the news website of the Julius-Maximilians University Würzburg.
